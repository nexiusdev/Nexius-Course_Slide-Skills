# Deck Review Principles For Training Illustrations

Use this reference after reviewing presentation-template sources and before
building or revising a training deck. It exists to prevent repetitive
illustration-panel decks and to keep teaching words editable.

## Durable Lessons

- Match visual form to teaching job. Use a person/scene illustration for human
  behavior and business context; use a diagram for relationships and workflows;
  use a chart for comparison, scale, or evidence; use a table or matrix for
  decisions and criteria; use a canvas preview for exercises.
- Not every slide needs an illustration. A rule, question, quote, discussion
  prompt, chart, or clean framework may be the strongest teaching object.
- Never trap teaching words in a bitmap. Keep slide titles, labels, callouts,
  chart labels, process names, and explanatory text as editable PowerPoint
  elements.
- Use image assets as separate visual layers. If the image contains useful text,
  recreate the text in PowerPoint and remove, crop, mask, or replace the text in
  the image where practical.
- Avoid one-note decks. A polished course deck should have rhythm: section
  openers, image-led concepts, diagram-led explanations, data/chart slides,
  comparison slides, activity/canvas slides, discussion prompts, and recap
  slides.
- Review rendered contact sheets for visual monotony. If more than 4-5 adjacent
  slides use the same layout pattern, redesign the sequence.

## Source Evidence Used For These Principles

- Slidesgo Effective Workflow: editable graphics include graphs, maps, tables,
  timelines, mockups, icons, and Storyset concepts.
- Slidesgo Process pages: process and flow diagram templates are designed to
  make workflows and decisions easier to understand.
- SlideModel illustration page: PowerPoint illustrations include diagrams,
  standalone images, metaphors, and infographics; visuals should aid clarity,
  memory, and balance.
- Envato template guidance: good templates include coordinated layouts,
  typography, charts, diagrams, image placeholders, and visual hierarchy.
