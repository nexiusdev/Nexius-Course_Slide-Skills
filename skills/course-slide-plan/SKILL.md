# Course Slide Plan Skill

## Purpose

Create a build-ready slide plan for a training deck. The plan should help a
future deck builder create slides with clear teaching purpose, short visible
text, useful visuals, and trainer notes that carry the detail.

## Use When

Use this skill when asked for:

- course slide descriptions,
- a PowerPoint deck plan,
- a slide-by-slide course outline,
- a revised slide sequence,
- a review deck blueprint,
- a course deck before actual PowerPoint production.

## Design Rules

- One slide teaches one thing.
- Visible text stays short and projection-safe.
- Detail belongs in speaker notes, templates, or handouts.
- Concept clusters end with a summary or overview slide.
- Visuals must help the trainer explain, not decorate.
- Speaker notes must prepare a new trainer to teach the slide.

## Slide Fields

For every slide, include:

- `Purpose`: the teaching job of the slide.
- `Visible content`: the exact or near-exact on-slide text.
- `Visual treatment`: diagram, cards, matrix, workflow, dashboard, image, or
  other layout guidance.
- `Illustration need`: recommended, optional, or not needed.
- `Speaker-note direction`: what the trainer must explain.

## Workflow

1. Read the course program and approval constraints.
2. Divide the course into orientation, concept sections, labs, and close.
3. Create a slide sequence with section dividers only when they help teaching
   rhythm.
4. Split dense concepts into separate slides.
5. Add summary slides after multi-slide concept clusters.
6. Mark visual needs deliberately.
7. Make activity slides explicit about task, timebox, output, and readout.

## Readability Rules

- Prefer one headline plus up to three short points.
- Avoid long comma-separated lists.
- Move field lists and detailed instructions into notes or handouts.
- Use diagrams for workflows, routing, governance, architecture, and maturity.
- Use matrices for comparisons and risk/control reviews.

## Verification

Check that:

- every slide has a purpose,
- no slide carries multiple unrelated concepts,
- visible content can be understood quickly,
- concept clusters have summaries,
- trainer-note direction exists for every slide,
- activity slides specify participant output,
- the plan can realistically become a large-room deck.
